Rooftop Wanderlust

The room at Folks Home where I’m staying is, in essence, a rooftop. Because of that, I always feel like I’m living on a rooftop — even when I’m lying in bed asleep.

Its specialty is the wind. Wind from the Saigon River, wind from the highway, and wind from the apartment blocks of Thảo Điền. Its oddity is that it has three windows, opening onto three views that, to me, feel like “Saigon at its most Saigon”: one looks out to the Saigon River (with its descending sunsets), one faces the Saigon Bridge / Hà Nội Highway (with traffic flowing 24/7), and one opens to the lush green of Thảo Điền.

Because of this, all at once I can wander through the lives of people from everywhere: the crowds at the Saigon Bridge junction, the red and blue container ships, rooftops glittering with lights, half-finished construction sites, poor neighborhoods with murky water, roaring supercars, and even the lonely sex workers waiting for customers.

Incredibly vibrant.

It gives me a feeling of living nomadically — something that, perhaps deep down, I belong to. I cannot stay in one place forever. Partly because circumstances haven’t yet allowed me to settle, partly because maybe I don’t want to. I like views that change. Different street corners. New neighborhoods. And above all, not breathing the same air for too long.

So even though I’ve only been in Saigon for five years, I’ve lived in many kinds of places, across quite a few districts, through many layers of the city. I once stayed in a small loft in District 4, where every night there were shouts and sounds of people chasing one another. I lived in a machine workshop in District 12, where I could fall asleep beside piles of equipment and engine oil. I also lived alone in a villa in Phú Mỹ Hưng — a place that always felt cold and soulless despite warm blankets and soft mattresses. I stayed in a rental apartment in Thảo Điền, where Westerners and locals mixed together. From apartment blocks to boarding houses, mezzanines, working-class areas, villa neighborhoods, poor alleys…

Each place is an experience that cannot be separated from who I am. Fortunately, they have allowed me to see the many colors of life. To understand those who struggle the most — to live with them, eat with them, befriend them. And to understand the wealthy as well. In the end, I’ve realized something: everyone is the same. Myself included — and everyone else.

The rich are not necessarily more dignified than the poor. And the poor are not necessarily less complex than the rich. I see myself in all of them. They are me, and I am no better or worse than they are.

These days of rooftop nomadic living arrive in a February sort of mood — weary, unanchored. Even as the month is nearly over, plans still drift without direction, nothing quite falling into place.

Looking back, that floating weariness feels very human. Waking at midnight, sitting idly, watching Grab drivers pass by, hearing the soft scraping sounds of street cleaners, the distant rumble of trucks moving through the night.

Nomadic living, after all, has its own charm. Things eventually find their way.

A windy home has its own joy — the wind chimes ringing lightly all day long.
