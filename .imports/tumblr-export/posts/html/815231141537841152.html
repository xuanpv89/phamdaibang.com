Some reflections from a “mind-travel” holiday

Last night I watched both Kon-Tiki (2012) and Kon-Tiki (1950), about Thor and his crew sailing nearly 8,000 km across the ocean on a primitive wooden raft in 1947, to recreate a voyage from over a thousand years earlier. Their goal was to prove that ancient people could have traveled such vast distances by sea without modern tools—that South Americans might once have migrated to remote Pacific islands (even though modern science has since shown this to be unlikely). Though his hypothesis turned out to be wrong, the experiment itself—drifting with winds and currents on a crude raft—was a remarkable success.

What’s interesting is that Thor wasn’t really an explorer before this journey. In fact, he was afraid of water and couldn’t swim (understandable, since he was more of a researcher than an adventurer). Yet he still dared to take on such a risky, almost reckless expedition.

You’d expect the journey to be full of danger and drama, like the 2012 film portrays—with all sorts of intense hardships. But the actual documentary footage he filmed during the trip tells a completely different story. It feels more like a 101-day adventure of a bunch of guys drifting on a wooden raft. For over 100 days, there were hardly any major dramatic events—or at least, none that felt dramatic to them.

The footage shows them enjoying the trip like childhood friends building banana rafts to cross rivers and go fishing. Sharks didn’t hunt them—they hunted sharks, just to see what they tasted like. They brought along a parrot, taught it random words, joking that it would soon become a real seabird. They had a guitar, sang songs, swam when it got too hot, even improvised a cage to dive safely away from sharks. They drank nearly 200 coconuts (still saving one to plant on land, even though they weren’t sure they’d make it). Each of them wore nothing but shorts, drifting at sea, inventing all sorts of things to pass the time.

In the end, they crossed a dangerous reef near an island—what they considered the hardest part of the journey—with their raft nearly falling apart… and reached shore safely. No emotional embraces like in the movie. Just casually carrying boxes and a guitar on their shoulders, planting their last coconut, looking for fresh coconut water on the island, exploring the archipelago, listening to Hawaiian music, and watching local girls dance in bikinis like mermaids.

What seemed like a grueling expedition turned out to be… just a long beach trip. At least in their minds. Either they were incredibly lucky, or just blissfully naive. Sometimes, “the ocean favors the naive.”

Life often feels like that. Before a seemingly difficult journey, people fall into two extremes: excessive fear or excessive idealism. Maybe it’s a kind of survival bias—many dreamers like that may have perished without anyone remembering—but in the end, there’s never a perfectly correct answer to what people calculate. Fearful people can succeed, and so can dreamers. Or you can be both—fearful inside, dreamy outside.

That said, no naive person succeeds forever. Luck doesn’t favor those who are unprepared.

These past few days, I’ve been fascinated by survival skills here and there. Like how some people can build an igloo entirely from snow, sleeping in the middle of a vast frozen landscape using just their hands and a simple homemade “knife.” The key is understanding nature. They might seem primitive compared to the modern world, but put on snow goggles and they suddenly look like cool fashion icons. Wandering around, poking at snow to find the right hardness, building shelters like farmers stacking bricks.

Modern people with the internet, who think they’re at the top of the world, sometimes study endlessly and still aren’t nearly as impressive. Like someone in the film said: if the natives tell you to do something, just follow exactly—if they say use wood, use wood, not steel; if they say use branches, use branches, not knives; if they say navigate by the stars, then do it—don’t rely on GPS (okay, I made that last part up…). But the idea stands: intelligence exists in many forms and places. Wisdom doesn’t arise on its own—it’s inherited.

This morning I started reading Prisoners of Geography by Tim Marshall, and found geopolitics, geography, and all things “geo” quite fascinating. Some people can build submarines diving over 4,000 meters beneath the Arctic Ocean to plant a titanium flag claiming territory. Others take road trips to Alaska and stand there looking across at a rival nation that seems far away, yet is visible to the naked eye despite being on another continent.

Some say the next world war might begin from a tiny white ice cap on a small blue-green speck drifting in the solar system. From a cosmic perspective, it’s all just tiny nuclear reactions, like those inside atomic nuclei. And each of us, sitting here staring at a screen, might be so small that science hasn’t even figured out what kind of “particle” we are. Yet each of us still imagines ourselves as vast as the universe.

In the end, every human is still just a naive dreamer—or a dreaming fool.
